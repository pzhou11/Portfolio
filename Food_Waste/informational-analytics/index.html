Introduction
According to the National Resources Defense Council, one-third of all food globally is either misused or wasted. And the numbers are even more shocking in developed nations such as the United States, where the food industry produces more than four times the amount of food needed by the population. Hence, most of the food produced is thrown to waste. In addition to being an economic loss, producing this huge surplus leads to deforestation, depleted water supplies, massive fossil fuel consumption, and biodiversity loss. Irrigation used to grow food that is thrown away could meet the domestic water needs of nine billion people. One year of food lost on the farm also equals about 300 million barrels of oil.

TYPES OF WASTE
In North America, more than 35% of the cereals, 60% of root and tuber crops, 20% of pulses and oilseeds, 52% of fruits and vegetables, 27% of Meat, 50% of seafood and fish and 20% of Dairy products produced is wasted. Food is wasted throughout the Food Supply Chain, from initial agricultural production down to final household consumption. In medium- and high-income countries such as USA, food is to a great extent wasted, meaning that it is thrown away even if it is still suitable for human consumption. Significant food loss and waste do, however, also occur early in the food supply chain. In low-income countries food is mainly lost during the early and middle stages of the food supply chain; much less food is wasted at the consumer level.

Economic Impacts
The total value of food loss at the retail and consumer levels was an estimated $161.6 billion in 2010. The two food groups with the highest value of losses were meat, poultry, and fish ($48.5 billion) and vegetables ($30 billion). These estimates are based on the value of foods as purchased at retail prices. When the total value of food loss at the consumer level in 2010 is broken down by food group, the meat, poultry, and fish group comprises almost a third (30 percent) of the total, because foods in this group tend to cost more per pound than many other foods. $161 billion dollar food loss is equivalent to GDP of certain countries (Romania, Pakistan) and if it were to be ranked against GDP of all countries it will be ranked 48th across 193 countries.

Environment Impacts
The global carbon footprint, excluding land use change, has been estimated at 3.3 Gtonnes of CO2 equivalent in 2007. If integrated into a country ranking of top emitters,food wastage would appear third, after USA and China, according to the latest data available (WRI 2012).This amount is more than twice the total GHG emissions of all USA road transportation in 2010 (1.5 Gtonnes of CO2 eq.). As per World Resources Institute 2014 estimates, USA emits 267 Million Metric tonnes of CO2 equivalent gases into atmosphere due to waste. California, Texas, Florida and New york being the largest contributors. The major contributors to the carbon footprint are meats, dairy and vegetables. Products of animal origin account altogether for about 33 percent of total carbon footprint,whereas their contribution to food wastage volumes is only 15 percent.
